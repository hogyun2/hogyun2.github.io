PhD Journey: Reflections on Growth and Challenges

March 10, 2026 Personal 5 min read

As I approach the later stages of my PhD journey in robotics, I find myself reflecting on the path that brought me here and the lessons learned along the way. When I first started this journey, I had certain expectations about what research would be like, but the reality has been both more challenging and more rewarding than I imagined.

The Beginning: Excitement and Uncertainty

Starting my PhD at Inha University felt like stepping into a vast unknown. Coming from a Naval Architecture background, transitioning into robotics and AI felt like learning an entirely new language. I remember the first few months being overwhelmed by the sheer amount of literature to read, concepts to understand, and skills to develop.

The excitement was palpable though. Every paper I read opened up new questions, and every conversation with my advisor Prof. Younggun Cho sparked new ideas. Being part of SPARO Lab introduced me to a community of passionate researchers who would become not just colleagues, but mentors and friends.

The Reality Check: When Research Gets Real

The honeymoon phase didn't last long. About a year into my PhD, I encountered my first major research roadblock. My initial approach to multi-robot SLAM wasn't working as expected, and I spent months debugging code, re-reading papers, and questioning my approach. This was my first real lesson: research is not a linear path.

"Research is not about being right the first time; it's about being persistent enough to find the right answer eventually."

Those difficult months taught me resilience. I learned to view failures not as setbacks, but as stepping stones toward better solutions. Each failed experiment provided valuable data, each rejected idea led to a better one.

Finding My Research Voice

As my understanding deepened, I began to develop my own research perspective. Working on SOLiD and ReFeree allowed me to explore different aspects of robotics - from LiDAR place recognition to radar-based localization. Each project taught me something new:

  • SOLiD taught me the importance of efficiency in resource-constrained environments
  • ReFeree showed me how unconventional sensors could solve complex problems
  • Commerge reinforced the value of communication-efficient algorithms in multi-robot systems

These projects weren't just technical achievements; they were opportunities to develop my identity as a researcher focused on scalable and collaborative robot autonomy.

The Importance of Community

One of the most valuable aspects of this journey has been the research community. Attending conferences like ICRA and IROS, presenting my work, and receiving feedback from experts worldwide has been incredibly enriching. The robotics community is remarkably supportive, and I've received invaluable advice from researchers at various career stages.

Being selected as a student representative for the Safety, Security, and Rescue Robotics (SSRR) community was particularly meaningful. It gave me a platform to contribute beyond my own research and help shape the future of our field.

Balancing Depth and Breadth

One ongoing challenge has been balancing depth in my specific research area with the breadth needed to be a well-rounded researcher. While diving deep into multi-robot SLAM, I've also tried to stay informed about related areas like computer vision, machine learning, and control theory.

This balance is crucial because robotics is inherently interdisciplinary. The best solutions often come from combining insights from different fields, and having that broader perspective has made me a better researcher.

Looking Forward

As I progress through my PhD, I'm excited about the future directions of my research. The field of multi-robot systems is evolving rapidly, with new challenges in areas like human-robot interaction, ethical AI, and deployment in real-world scenarios.

I'm particularly interested in how we can make multi-robot systems more accessible and practical for everyday use. This means continuing to work on communication-efficient algorithms while also considering user experience, safety, and social acceptance.

Advice for Future PhD Students

If I could go back and give advice to my younger self starting this journey, here's what I would say:

  • Embrace uncertainty - Not knowing everything is normal and expected
  • Build relationships - Your network is as important as your knowledge
  • Learn from failures - Every setback is a learning opportunity
  • Stay curious - The best research comes from genuine curiosity
  • Take care of yourself - Mental and physical health are crucial for long-term success

The PhD journey is not easy, but it's incredibly rewarding. It's a process of not just conducting research, but of becoming a researcher. It shapes how you think, how you approach problems, and how you see the world.

I'm grateful for this opportunity to contribute to the field of robotics and excited to see where this journey takes me next. The challenges continue, but so does the excitement of discovery and the satisfaction of pushing the boundaries of what's possible.
